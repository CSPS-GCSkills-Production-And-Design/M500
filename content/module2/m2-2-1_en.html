Complaint Bodies

Given the volume and complexity of contracts awarded and administered by the federal government, it is inevitable that issues or complaints should arise.

Normally, when an issue or complaint is raised by a supplier or potential supplier, the contracting officer in the relevant department or agency is contacted to discuss the concern. Some departments have also established their own internal dispute-resolution processes. If the complaint cannot be resolved by the department or agency, the supplier or potential supplier can contact one of the procurement-complaint bodies listed below. Which body has the jurisdiction will depend on the nature of the issue in question, among other factors, including the dollar value of the contract.

Competition Bureau

The Competition Bureau, an independent law enforcement agency headed by the Commissioner of Competition, ensures that Canadian businesses and consumers prosper in a competitive and innovative marketplace. The Bureau is responsible for the administration and enforcement of the following acts. To learn more about each one, consult the Competition Bureau’s website.

  • Competition Act
  • Consumer Packaging and Labelling Act
  • Textile Labelling Act
  • Precious Metals Marking Act

Complaints examined by the Competition Bureau must fall within the scope of these acts.

The basic operating assumption of the Competition Bureau is that competition is good for both business and consumers.

Price fixing, bid rigging, misleading representations, abuse of dominant position, mergers, etc., —these are all types of anti-competitive activities investigated by the Bureau.

Canadian International Trade Tribunal (CITT)

The Canadian International Trade Tribunal (CITT) is the bid challenge authority for federal government procurement covered by any of the trade agreements.

On occasion, a potential supplier may file a complaint concerning a procurement action to the CITT, on the grounds that any aspect of the procurement process relating to a requirement covered by these agreements is unfair or discriminatory. For example, a supplier may have reason to believe that a contract has been or is about to be awarded improperly or illegally, or that in some way they have been wrongfully denied a contract or an opportunity to compete for one.

The CITT is authorized to receive complaints pertaining to any aspect of the procurement process up to and including the awarding of the contract, and also to conduct inquiries and make determinations.

When the tribunal determines that a complaint is valid, it may recommend to the government such remedy as it considers appropriate, including:

  • That a new solicitation for the designated contract be issued;
  • That the bids be re‑evaluated;
  • That the designated contract be terminated;
  • That the designated contract be awarded to the complainant; or
  • That the complainant be compensated for lost profits or loss of an amount specified by the tribunal.

The impact of the CITT on the government procurement process has been to enforce rigid rules ensuring a fair, competitive, and transparent procurement process.

Office of the Procurement Ombudsman  (OPO)

The following provides detailed information about these services.

The Procurement Ombudsman reports to the Minister of PSPC and is mandated to:

  • Review complaints with respect to the awarding of a contract for the acquisition of goods below $26,400 and services below $105,700 (including taxes);
  • Review complaints with respect to the administration of a contract, regardless of dollar value;
  • Review the practices of departments for acquiring goods and services and make recommendations where necessary to improve those practices;
  • Ensure that an alternative dispute-resolution process is provided, if both parties agree to participate;
  • Provide an alternative dispute-resolution service that:
    • helps the parties to the contract understand each other’s views;
    • focuses on resolving the dispute in a quick, positive and respectful manner;
    • costs less and is less time-consuming than court proceedings;
    • allows the parties full control over outcomes (no decision is “imposed”); and
    • leads to a mutual agreement on a legally binding settlement.
Federal Court

Suppliers may also file a complaint in court when a requirement exceeds the Office of the Procurement Ombudsman’s thresholds, when a trade agreement does not apply (i.e. no CITT), or as further recourse to request a review of a CITT determination.
