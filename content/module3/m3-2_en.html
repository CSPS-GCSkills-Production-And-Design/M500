Contracting for Goods

Let’s take a closer look at “transaction authority” or the authority to enter into contracts. As identified in each department’s establishing act (the Act that legally establishes or constitutes a department), ministers have inherent power to contract for goods and services.

Although ministers responsible for departments have the inherent power to contract for goods and services, the Department of Public Works and Government Services Act (DPWGSA) supplants the authority of each minister to contract for goods. That authority, however, is restored to the extent determined by the Minister of PSPC through a formal delegation.

Since 2007, the Minister of PSPC has provided:

  • A standard $25,000 delegation of authority for the procurement of goods to colleague ministers responsible for other departments; and
  • A higher delegation of authority for the procurement of goods when departments use PSPC standing offers or supply arrangements and when goods are required as a result of a pressing emergency.

A “pressing emergency” is defined as being an actual or imminent life-threatening situation, a disaster which endangers the quality of life or has resulted in the loss of life, or one that may result in significant loss or damage to Crown property. In addition, emergency contracting is always limited to the most senior delegated official available in the department.

  1. the amount of the contract including amendments does not exceed $25,000 (standard delegation of authority);
  2. the amount of the contract exceeds $25,000 but it is a call-up on a standing offer (standing offer);
  3. the amount of the contract exceeds $25,000 but under exceptional circumstances (special delegation of authority).

Did you know?

Other special or exceptional delegation of authorities for the procurement of specific goods can also be granted by the Minister of PSPC to colleague ministers upon formal request.

Standard Delegation

Standard Delegation

Standard $25,000 delegation of authority for goods from the Minister of Public Services and Procurement Canada.

Example: Using the standard PSPC $25,000 delegation of authority for goods, Library and Archives Canada purchases a $13,000 scanner that is not available via a standing offer.

Standing Offer

Standing Offer

Delegation of authority from the Minister of Public Services and Procurement Canada, up to the call up limitation of a PSPC standing offer.

Example: Using a PSPC standing offer, the Canadian Food Inspection Agency purchases $77,000 worth of lab equipment from a standing offer holder to replace some obsolete or defective equipment in one of their laboratories. The value of the purchase is greater than the standard PSPC $25,000 delegation, but still within the $80,000 call‑up limitation of the PSPC standing offer for lab equipment.

Special Delegation

Special Delegation

Special delegation of authority for the procurement of specific goods from the Minister of Public Services and Procurement Canada to a colleague minister.

Example: Using a special delegation of authority from the Minister of PSPC, Transport Canada purchases a 2015 Lexus IS250 for $39,000 from a local dealership (off the lot) for vehicle testing purposes at the Transport Canada vehicle testing centre in Blainville, Quebec. Normally, government vehicles are purchased by PSPC on behalf of client departments by issuing call-ups against PSPC standing offers put in place directly with car manufacturers. In this case, however, Transport Canada had received a special delegation from the Minister of PSPC to purchase vehicles—strictly for testing purposes—directly off the lots and not from manufacturers through PSPC standing offers. These test vehicles are purchased from dealerships, rather than directly from manufacturers, to make sure they’re not specially “prepped” and are the same as a consumer would get.
