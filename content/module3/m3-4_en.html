Contracting Limits

No matter the source of the authority to enter into contracts, ministers cannot enter into or amend contracts that exceed the limits contained in the Treasury Board Contracts Directive (which is also known as Appendix C of the Treasury Board Contracting Policy) without first obtaining the approval of the Treasury Board.

In Appendix C – Part I of the Treasury Board Contracting Policy, you will notice in each table (schedules 1, 2 and 3) how the authority to enter into and amend contracts varies depending on the sourcing method.

Select each of the following tabs to learn more about each method.

Electronic Bidding

The authority to enter into and amend contracts is greater when using the electronic bidding approach. The electronic tendering approach is where a notice of proposed procurement is published on the Government Electronic Tendering Service (GETS) website so that all potential suppliers can see the opportunity and submit a bid.

Competitive

The authority to enter into and amend contracts decreases when using the competitive approach—which means directly inviting selected suppliers to submit a bid.

Non‑competitive

Lastly, the authority to enter into and amend contracts is further decreased when a non‑competitive approach is used—awarding a sole–source contract.

The Treasury Board Contracting Policy and the Treasury Board Contracts Directive limit the dollar value for contracts with Former public servant  in receipt of a pension. Non-competitive contracts with former public servants in receipt of a pension cannot exceed $25,000 (including all amendments). Treasury Board approval is required to enter into or amend a contract whose total dollar value exceeds $25,000. Competitive contracts (including electronic bidding method) with former public servants in receipt of a pension cannot exceed $100,000 (including all amendments). Treasury Board approval is required to enter into or amend a competitive contract with former public servants in receipt of a pension whose total value exceeds $100,000.

Finally, the Treasury Board Contracts Directive also provides other departmental exceptional contracting limits.

Select each tab to view some examples.

Example 1

The Minister of Public Services and Procurement Canada may enter into or amend a contract to repair and overhaul military equipment if the contract, including any amendments thereto, does not exceed $50 million.

Example 2

The Minister of Canadian Heritage may enter into or amend a contract to purchase historical material if the total under the contract, including any amendments thereto, does not exceed $150,000.

Example 3

The Minister of Foreign Affairs, for the purpose of Canadian missions abroad, may enter into and amend competitive security service contracts if the amount does not exceed $3.75 million.

Example 4

The Minister of Health Canada may, for the purpose of teaching and research services related to its operations, enter into a non‑competitive service contract with a contractor, including one with a former official of the public service in receipt of a pension, if the amount does not exceed $100,000.

In conclusion, it is important to note that when the estimated dollar value of a procurement for goods or services exceeds a department’s delegated authority or the department’s contracting limits identified in the Treasury Board Contracting Limits, the department must either send a requisition to PSPC in order for PSPC to conduct the procurement on the department’s behalf, or obtain approval from Treasury Board (via a TB Submission) to conduct the procurement on its own.
