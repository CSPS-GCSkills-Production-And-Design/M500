Common Service Organizations (CSO)

A vital aspect in government contracting is the role played by Common Service Organizations (CSOs) and their relationships with departments at all stages in the life-cycle management of goods and services. When the interests of the government can best be met by centralized acquisition, the Common Services Policy provides that procurement be carried out by the designated mandatory CSO unless the services are designated optional or an exception has been granted.

The Common Services Policy lists the Government of Canada’s Common Services Organizations (CSOs), and enumerates the optional and mandatory services these organizations provide.

Public Services and Procurement Canada (PSPC)

PSPC operates as a common service organization providing both optional and mandatory acquisition services to departments, boards, and agencies of the Government of Canada in support of their program objectives.

It is important to note that PSPC has exclusive responsibility for the procurement of goods, as set out in the Department of Public Works and Government Services Act.

PSPC is an optional service provider for procurement of services such as the asset management of movables, contracting for printing, publishing, public warehousing, etc. However, under the Common Services Policy, PSPC is a mandatory provider of various procurement-related services, including:

  • Research and development contracting;
  • Executive vehicle acquisitions;
  • Guard services;
  • Co-ordination of, and contracting for, advertising and paid announcements;
  • Co-ordination of, and contracting for, public opinion research;
  • Shared travel services initiative;
  • Central removal (relocation of employees); and
  • Film, video and multimedia production.

In addition to procurement services, PSPC also provides other services such as accommodation, translation, banking, to name a few.

To learn more about these services, you are welcome to visit the PSPC’s website.

Depending on your department's delegation of authority and the goods or services you will be asked to buy, there will be situations where you need to work with PSPC.

When dealing with PSPC, you will work closely with their COs to develop responsive, creative, and flexible procurement strategies to meet your client’s needs and their operational objectives. Having PSPC handle the requirement on your organization’s behalf makes them accountable for the integrity of the procurement process and for awarding the contract. However, it is important to note that the client remains accountable for the integrity of the requirement definition (Statements of Work).

Did you know?

Other departments and agencies may only procure goods when an appropriate delegation of authority has been made by the Minister of Public Services and Procurement Canada (PSPC).

Public Services and Procurement Canada (PSPC) – Acquisitions Program

The PSPC Acquisitions Program is divided in two branches—the Defence and Marine Procurement Branch and the Procurement Branch. Each branch handles the acquisition of specific groups of goods, services, and construction. Purchases range from office supplies to military vessels to security systems, and everything in between. The Acquisitions Program aims to:

  • Provide departments and agencies with expert assistance at each stage of the supply cycle (i.e. contracting officers, policy analysts, subject-matter experts, cost analysts, etc.);
  • Offer tools that simplify and accelerate the acquisition of goods and services (e.g. standing offers and supply arrangements);
  • Ensure the government exercises due diligence and maintains the integrity of the procurement process;
  • Provide clients with comprehensive services for acquiring complex and commercial goods and services;
  • Develop, implement and maintain policies, tool kits and standards (i.e. green procurement checklist, security requirements checklist);
  • Provide operational advice on and develop innovative electronic procurement approaches;
  • Provide assistance in identifying requirements and carrying out all the steps in the procurement process, including:
    • Identifying the goods or services to be purchased;
    • Selecting the most effective procurement approach;
    • Developing appropriate evaluation criteria;
    • Requesting, receiving, and evaluating bids;
    • Negotiating contracts;
    • Debriefing unsuccessful bidders; and
    • Administering contracts.
  • Seek innovative services and alternative forms of service delivery; and
  • Manage quality assurance, performance-review, and performance-monitoring indicators.

Public Services and Procurement Canada (PSPC) Regional Offices

Regional offices of PSPC carry out departmental activities across Canada. From a supply perspective, PSPC regional offices provide services to other departments at the local level. These services include technical, industrial and commercial acquisitions, scientific, marine, engineering and professional services acquisitions, the administration of standing offers, the maintenance and repair of office equipment and furniture, and disposal of materiel.

PSPC also maintains offices in the U.S. (Washington, D.C.) and in Europe (Koblenz, Germany), primarily to meet the needs of the military.

Shared Services Canada (SSC)

SSC has expanded its mandate beyond delivering mandated email, data centre and network services to partner organizations, in a consolidated and standardized manner, in support of Government of Canada programs and services. The department now consolidates government-wide procurement of software and hardware for workplace technology devices (e.g. printers, and desktop and laptop computers).

Consult SSC’s website to learn more.

Department of Justice Canada

The Department of Justice Canada is responsible for the legal affairs of the government as a whole and for providing legal services to individual departments and agencies. These services include legal advice, preparing legal documents, drafting legislation, regulating or conducting litigation, and overseeing all legal mechanisms used to achieve the government’s overall objectives.

The Government Contracts Regulations require that contracts for the performance of legal services may be entered into only by or under the authority of the Minister of Justice. Accordingly, while it is recognized that lawyers may be engaged for purposes other than the performance of legal services, the Department of Justice must be consulted in every case where a contract for services is entered into with a practising member of the bar, to determine whether the contract is for legal services and thus whether the approval of the Department of Justice is required. No contracting authority may enter into a contract for services with a practising member of the bar without first having obtained the Department of Justice’s approval, or having been advised by the Department of Justice that the contract does not involve legal services.

Consult the Department of Justice Canada website to learn more.

Global Affairs Canada (GAC)

Global Affairs Canada is responsible for negotiating trade agreements on behalf of the Government of Canada, including the negotiation of government procurement obligations. GAC is also responsible for the procurement of goods, services and constructions services in support of diplomatic and consular missions as well as other departments and agencies operating outside Canada.

Consult the GAC’s website to learn more.
