Scenario 2

Requirement for Independent Review of the Accounts Payable Processes

You are assigned a contract request from the director general (DG) responsible for the Finance and Administration Branch in your department, who requires the services of a consultant to conduct an independent review of the accounts payable processes in the department. The DG estimates that this review could be achieved by one expert consultant and that a report, including findings and recommendations, could be delivered within two months of the start of the contract. The DG knows that a former director from another department (former public servant in receipt of a pension) is qualified and available immediately to perform this task at a per‑diem rate of $1,200. The DG estimates that given the per‑diem rate and expected duration of the contract, the value of the contract would be just over $50,000.

Write your answer and justification in the space provided below, then compare it with the feedback. You may consult the reference material provided.

Question  of 

As the contracting officier, could you award a $50,000 contract directly to any supplier without competition (i.e. a sole-source contract)? Provide the rationale for your decision.

The answer is: No. In this situation, you cannot award a $50,000 contract directly to a supplier as no sole source justification was provided by your client and reference was not made to any of the Government Contracts Regulations’ four exceptions to set aside the need to solicit bids. Without such justifications, bids would have to be solicited first and the contract awarded to the successful bidder (based on pre‑established evaluation criteria and contractor selection method).

The Government Contracts Regulations (GCRs) prescribe the conditions for entering into Crown procurement contracts and for dealing with financial security.

Part I of the Government Contracts Regulations describes the four exceptions to set aside the need to solicit bids.

Part I of the GCRs also provides for the making of advance and progress payments. Part II of the GCRs sets out conditions for bid and contract security and Part III sets out the terms that are deemed to be expressly specified in every bid solicitation and contract for construction, goods or services that provide for the payment of any money by Her Majesty.

Let’s assume that a competitive bidding process is undertaken to meet the DG’s requirement and five firms  have submitted a bid. On bid closing day, you pick up the bids from your department’s bid receiving unit. Upon opening and examining the bids, you notice the following:

  • Two bids from two different bidders are in the same envelope. The proposed prices from the two bidders are significantly higher than what was originally anticipated considering the level of effort.
  • Two other bids, submitted separately, contain identical irregularities (same spelling mistakes and same miscalculations). Their proposed prices are also significantly higher than what was originally anticipated based on the level of effort.
  • A fifth bid is submitted in the prescribed format and it meets all of the mandatory criteria. This bidder’s proposed price is lower than all the other bidders’ prices but still considerably higher than what was originally anticipated based on the level of effort.

What could these irregularities possibly suggest? What should you do about them?

The answer is: This could be a sign that bid-rigging is taking place. In this case, you must notify your immediate supervisor and Legal Services since there is an indication of possible bid-rigging activities, collusion or fraud.

Bid‑rigging refers to a secret agreement between suppliers in a bid solicitation process, so that one or more suppliers do not bid at all, or they agree on the prices and terms of their bids. Bid-rigging has serious negative economic consequences for businesses and for the public. It is also a crime. Canada’s Competition Act prohibits bid-rigging and provides for penalties, on conviction, of up to 14 years in jail and fines at the discretion of the courts.

The Competition Act is a federal law governing most business conduct in Canada. It contains both criminal and civil provisions aimed at preventing anti‑competitive practices in the marketplace.

The criminal provisions of the Competition Act include those covering conspiracies that unduly lessen competition, bid‑rigging, price discrimination, predatory pricing, and price maintenance.

Bid‑rigging can target bids in any sector: all products and services are at risk.

There are a number of warning signs that bids may be rigged. Contracting officers must be on guard throughout the procurement process. Visit the Competition Bureau’s toolbox for procurement on bid‑rigging: detecting, preventing and reporting to learn more.

When it is considered necessary, Legal Services will assist in subsequent discussions with the Competition Bureau Canada, the federal independent law enforcement agency responsible for the administration and enforcement of the Competition Act.

The Competition Bureau is the agency responsible for the administration and enforcement of the Competition Act. Headed by the Commissioner of Competition, the organization investigates anti-competitive practices and promotes compliance with the laws under its jurisdiction.

In addition, the Government Contracts Regulations include deemed terms that mitigate the risk of corruption and collusion in the bidding process.

As a result of a competitive bidding process, the winning bidder is a former public servant who owns their own firm. Could you award the contract to a former public servant in receipt of a pension? Provide the rationale for your decision.

Yes. In this situation, you would follow the regular approval processes.

Competitive and non-competitive service contracts with former public servants in receipt of a pension are subject to the minister’s basic contracting limits set out in Appendix C of the TB Contracting Policy.

However, for non-competitive service contracts, the department’s responsible minister must provide contract approval prior to entering into a contract with a former public servant in receipt of a pension when :

  • the FPS is in receipt of pension; AND the contract is over $200,000
    OR
  • the annualized rate of remuneration exceeds $200,000 and the contract term exceeds ninety (90) working days
    OR
  • the FPS is in receipt of lump sum payments* when total fees payable exceed $5,000 during the lump sum payment period.

*Lump‑sum payment

Former public servants in receipt of a lump-sum payment are those former employees who have received a lump-sum payment made pursuant to the Public Sector Compensation Act as provided in the Work Force Adjustment Directive (WFAD) or other TB employment transition programs, and former members of the Armed Forces and the Royal Canadian Mounted Police in receipt of lump-sum payments through corresponding force-reduction programs.

Competitive and non‑competitive contracts for the services of a former public servant in receipt of a lump‑sum payment are subject to a special remuneration policy whereby the total fee payable cannot exceed $5,000 during the lump‑sum payment period. This limit applies cumulatively to all contracts. The lump-sum payment period is defined as the period, measured in weeks of salary, for which payment has been made to facilitate the transition to retirement or to other employment. The lump-sum payment period does not include the period of severance pay, which is measured in a like manner.

Note: Contract requirements above the departmental limits are to be sent to a common service provider to be fulfilled using their authorities.

Let’s assume that part way through the contract, you receive a notice from Poyle & Smith Inc. (Licensed Insolvency Trustees) informing you that the small firm, the contractor, has filed for bankruptcy. As the contracting officer, what should you do?

The answer is: Upon receipt of a bankruptcy, receivership or insolvency notice or when there is an indication of such, the contracting officer must:

  • Inform the client (in this case, the DG);
  • Develop a plan, in consultation with the client, for completion of the work (i.e. moving to the next compliant bidder); and
  • Advise Legal Services of the situation.

The Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act is legislation governing insolvency and bankruptcy. It sets out the rights and responsibilities of all the individuals and institutions with an interest or involvement in the affairs of an insolvent or bankrupt person. These include the Superintendent of Bankruptcy, the receivers, the court, the trustees, the creditors, and the debtors.

This Act has an impact on procurement activities in that it identifies the rights and responsibilities of the Crown and its contractors and their subcontractors should a contractor or subcontractor become insolvent or bankrupt during the life of a Crown procurement contract.
