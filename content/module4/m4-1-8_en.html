Scenario 7

Requirement for Renovation of Shower Rooms

As a contracting officer for your department, you are responsible for conducting a construction procurement process for the renovation of shower rooms located in one of the department’s facilities in downtown Ottawa. The project includes the following work:

  1. Demolition of existing men's and women's washrooms and janitor’s room (walls, flooring and doors).
  2. Construction of new washrooms with shower/locker facilities and new janitor's room (new walls, flooring and doors with frames; painting, ceramic tile, lockers, benches and epoxy flooring).
  3. Installation of new plumbing fixtures.
  4. Modifications to existing ventilation system including installation of ductwork, grilles, and controls and sprinkler modifications.
  5. Demolition of existing systems, including but not limited to lighting and power.
  6. Provision of new systems, including but not limited to lighting and power.
  7. Provide certification, verification and commissioning of all systems upon job completion.

The estimated dollar value of this requirement is well within your department’s delegation of authority for construction contracts.

Write your answer and justification in the space provided below, then compare it with the feedback. You may consult the reference material provided.

Question 1 of 3

In your bid solicitation document, you requested that bidders submit a bid security (financial) along with their bid. A bond or deposit is given to guarantee entry into a contract. This bond/deposit is given to indemnify Canada against increased costs if the bidder does not carry out the specified undertaking to enter into a contract.

At bid closing time, six bids had been received. All bids received included the required security deposit (certified cheques). Your client informs you that he will not be in a position to award a contract for the work until three months from now. Until the contract is awarded, should you cash and deposit the certified cheques submitted by the bidders? Provide the rationale for your decision.

The answer is: No, you should not cash and deposit the certified cheques. A certified cheque submitted as a security deposit with a tender for a contract is to be held uncashed until the successful bid is selected or for up to one year, whichever comes first.

As stated in Part II of the Government Contracts Regulations, security deposits received from bidders must be dealt with as follow:

“12 A contracting authority shall hold any bid bond, payment bond, performance bond or non-negotiable security deposit that is received by the authority until the terms of the security are fulfilled.

  1. 13 (1) Where a negotiable security deposit is received by a contracting authority as bid security, the contracting authority shall hold that security deposit until it can be disposed of in accordance with subsection (2) or (3).
  2. (2) Where a bid is accepted and contract security is required to be provided,
    1. (a) the contracting authority shall continue to hold the security deposit referred to in subsection (1) until the contract security is provided; and
    2. (b) when the contract security is provided, the contracting authority shall return the security deposit referred to in subsection (1) to the person from whom it was received unless it forms part of the contract security, in which case it shall be retained as contract security.
  3. (3) Where a bid is rejected, or is accepted but contract security is not required, the contracting authority shall return the security deposit referred to in subsection (1) to the person from whom it was received. ”

Let’s assume that, during the course of executing the work, the successful bidder (the contractor) will require access to secure work sites. Should this be a concern?

The answer is: Yes, this must be a concern to you and your client.

The objective of the Policy on Government Security , as it pertains to contracting, is to ensure that sensitive information and assets of the government are properly protected when entrusted to industry.

The project authority (your client) and the departmental security officer are responsible for ensuring that their department adhere to the policy and that provisions are made for any suppliers used to provide goods or services to ensure that they also meet the same requirements.

Departments are responsible for protecting sensitive information and assets under their control according to the security policy and its operational standards. This responsibility applies to all phases of the procurement process, including bidding, negotiating, awarding, performance and termination of contracts, as well as to internal government operations.

Whether a contract is within or outside a department's delegated contracting responsibilities, the department is responsible for identifying sensitive information and assets warranting safeguards.

The department has a choice for contracts that are within a department's delegated contracting responsibilities and that involve access to sensitive Canadian government information, assets and secure work sites. The department may itself ensure that the contractor meets the appropriate security requirements or request that PSPC perform this task.

For contracts that are outside a department’s delegated contracting responsibilities and that involve access to sensitive information and assets, PSPC is responsible for ensuring compliance with the appropriate security requirements. In this regard, PSPC is responsible for assessing threats and risks and for consulting with the department on risk management decisions that may involve either extraordinary risk or expense.

Security Requirements Check List

Departments must use the Security Requirements Check List (SRCL) to define the security requirements of contracts for which PSPC is the contracting authority. The SRCL should also be completed when a department retains contracting authority. This requirement also applies to contracts generated by supply arrangements and call‑ups against standing offers, when the supply arrangement/standing offer or contract/call‑up, or both, contain security requirements. The SRCL must be prepared with the assistance of the departmental security officer (DSO).

The completed Security Requirements Check List must accompany all requisitions and related contractual documents, including subcontracts, which contain security requirements. It does not replace the necessary clauses in the contract that specify security requirements. PSPC has developed standard security clauses for use in contracts having different requirements.

As well, when a department retains contracting authority, an SRCL should be completed.

SRCL and Subcontracting

It is the prime contractor’s responsibility to obtain approval from the Contract Security Program (CSP) before awarding a subcontract with security requirements. To do this, the prime contractor must submit an SRCL and a request for private sector organization screening (PSOS) to the CSP.

Each subcontract must have its own unique subcontract number and specific SRCL. Bear in mind that work cannot begin until the subcontract is approved by the CSP.

Security Requirements for Organization and Personnel

Contracting officers must take into account any industrial security requirement when developing the procurement strategy and also when determining the procurement schedule. Procurement schedules must take into account that the organization and personnel security screening process may take a considerable length of time. The contracting officer must provide as much lead time as possible to the departmental security officer and/or to the Canadian Industrial Security Directorate (CISD) to process organization and personnel security screenings, as well as to give time to bidders to register for the Contract Security Program (if required) and implement security recommendations before contract awarding.

Organization Security Screening

The approximate time frames for an organizational security screening are as follows:

  • Designated Organizational Screening (DOS): up to six months; and
  • Facility Security Clearance (FSC): six months or more.
Personnel Security Screening

The approximate time frames for completing a personnel security screening are as follows:

  • Simple Reliability Status: 7 business days upon receipt of a properly completed request;
  • Complex Reliability Status: 120 business days upon receipt of a properly completed request;
  • Classified Secret Clearance request: 75 business days (in addition to reliability screening timelines) upon receipt of a properly completed request.

Note: Reliability checks may be more complex and take additional time to process. This occurs when a security screening interview is required in order to assess any adverse information contained within a file.

All new security screening clearances, updates (renewals), or upgrades of personnel security screening clearances issued by PSPC are subject to the new criminal record check process and will require electronic fingerprints.

The new criminal record check process applies to the following levels of security screening requests:

  • Reliability Status
  • Secret
  • Top Secret

For more information and details on this new process, please visit the Contract Security Program website.

 

As a contracting officer, you are contacted by a potential industry contractor/supplier (private-sector organization, sole proprietor, corporation or foreign supplier) requiring sponsorship to bid on a sensitive government contract. What can you do?

The answer is: When potential suppliers (contractors and subcontractors) need to be sponsored into the Contract Security Program, as a contracting officer, you can act as a sponsor and submit a written request to the Contract Security Program to initiate the company’s (supplier’s) registration (clearance) process.

This request can be made in anticipation of opportunities to bid on future contracts with security requirements or when there is a strong possibility that the contractor and their personnel will require access to sensitive information, assets or secure sites at some future date.

As a contracting officer, you must be in constant contact with the client and the security officer, and you must consider the time it will take the Contract Security Program to grant an organization security clearance and issue personnel security clearances to their employees because there may be impacts on timelines for awarding of the contract. Don’t forget that if the suppliers need to have document safeguarding capability (DSC) or one of its subsets, information technology (IT) or production, there will also be an impact on timelines.

To be registered and screened through the Contract Security Program, organizations must first be sponsored by one of the following sources approved by the Government of Canada:

  • Federal government contracting officer
  • Federal government security officer
  • Federal government project officer

For subcontracts, an approved source may be a private-sector organization (also called a prime contractor) already registered in the Contract Security Program and working on active contracts. The only instance where a prime contractor can sponsor a subcontractor without a valid contract in place is when a major Crown project is involved and the client department confirms this project.

For help in navigating the sponsorship process, consult the PSPC Contract Security Program sponsorship roadmap.
