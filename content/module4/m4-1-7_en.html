Scenario 6

Requirement for Inspection of Bridges and Culverts

As a PSPC contracting officer in the Vancouver regional office, you receive a requisition to put in place a professional service contract to undertake a detailed comprehensive inspection of 56 bridges and five culverts under the jurisdiction of PSPC on the Alaska Highway in the Province of British Columbia. After posting a Notice of Proposed Procurement (NPP) on the Government Electronic Tendering System (GETS), three bids are received and properly evaluated. Once the financial proposals are opened, the successful structural engineering firm with the necessary expertise, capacity and resources to meet the project objectives is identified and awarded a $978,000 contract for the execution of the work over a three-year period.

Write your answer and justification in the space provided below, then compare it with the feedback. You may consult the reference material provided.

Question 1

Upon performing a thorough pre‑contract award examination (i.e. reviewing the past performance of the vendor) of the successful firm’s bid, it is found that the president of the firm is the brother of a federal government minister. Can the contract still be awarded to this firm? Provide the rationale for your decision.

The answer is: Upon properly briefing senior management and the Minister of PSPC, it is determined that the contract can in fact technically be awarded to this firm.

The Conflict of Interest Act states that:

No minister of the Crown, minister of state or parliamentary secretary who otherwise has the authority shall permit anyone acting on his or her behalf to enter into a contract with a spouse, common-law partner, child, sibling or parent of another minister of the Crown, minister of state or parliamentary secretary or party colleague in Parliament, except in accordance with an impartial administrative process in which the minister of the Crown, minister of state or parliamentary secretary plays no part.

The Conflict of Interest Act establishes conflict of interest and post-employment rules for public office holders. Public office holders are: ministers of the Crown, ministers of state, parliamentary secretaries, the chief electoral officer, members of ministerial staff, ministerial advisors, Governor in Council and ministerial appointees.

For the purposes of this Act, a public office holder is in a conflict of interest when he or she exercises an official power, duty or function that provides an opportunity to further his or her private interests or those of his or her relatives or friends or to improperly further another person’s private interests.

The Conflict of Interest Act further addresses procurement-related transactions in Section 13 – Contracts with public sector entities, and Section 14 – Contracting.

The Act’s Rules for Former Reporting Public Office Holders state that:

No former reporting public office holder shall enter into a contract of service with an entity with which he or she had direct and significant official dealings during the period of one year immediately before his or her last day in office. With respect to all former reporting public office holders except former ministers of the Crown and former ministers of state, this prohibition applies for the period of one year following the former reporting public office holder’s last day in office. With respect to former ministers of the Crown and former ministers of state, this prohibition applies for a period of two years following their last day in office.
