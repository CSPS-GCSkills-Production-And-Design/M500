Scenario 4

Requirement for Accounts Payable Audit (Corporate Lawyer)

Your manager gives you the file on a request for professional services. Upon examining the request, you notice that the director of accounts payable is requesting that a $10,000 non‑competitive contract be awarded to Paul Schwartz, a corporate lawyer, to perform an audit on two specific accounts payable files.

Write your answer and justification in the space provided below, then compare it with the feedback. You may consult the reference material provided.

Question  of 

Let’s assume that the director of accounts payable has provided a solid and acceptable justification for awarding a non‑competitive contract; that is, a justification that is in accordance with the requirements of the Contracting Policy and the Government Contracts Regulations for awarding non‑competitive contracts. In such a case, would you be able to proceed with awarding the contract to Paul Schwartz (the corporate lawyer)? Provide the rationale for your decision.

The answer is: In this situation, given the fact that Mr. Schwartz would not be performing legal services, but rather an audit on two accounts payable files under this contract, you would most likely be able to award the contract, after receiving approval from the Department of Justice.

The Government Contracts Regulations require that contracts for the performance of legal services may be entered into only by or under the authority of the Minister of Justice. Accordingly, while it is recognized that lawyers may be engaged for purposes other than the performance of legal services, the Department of Justice must be consulted in every case where a contract for services is entered into with a practising member of the bar, to determine whether the contract is for legal services and thus whether the approval of the Department of Justice is required. No contracting authority may enter into a contract for services with a practising member of the bar without first having obtained the approval of the Department of Justice, or having been advised by the Department of Justice that the contract does not involve legal services. Note that the Policy on Contracting for Legal Services and Legal Agent Appointment states that contracts for the performance of legal services entered into by or under the authority of the Minister of Justice are otherwise not subject to the requirements of the Government Contracts Regulations or the Treasury Board Contracting Policy.

Let’s assume that the Department of Justice grants you the authorization to award this $10,000 contract to Paul Schwartz (for purposes other than the performance of legal services). Dan Dean, another qualified supplier of auditing services, has been made aware of the contract awarded to Paul Schwartz and he is not happy that he was not considered for this contract. Dan Dean would now like to file an official complaint regarding the awarding of this contract. With whom should the complaint be filed?

The answer is: Dan Dean should file his complaint with the Office of the Procurement Ombudsman.

The Office of the Procurement Ombudsman (OPO) is an independent organization with a government-wide mandate. The position of Procurement Ombudsman was created by amendments to the Department of Public Works and Government Services Act enacted pursuant to the Federal Accountability Act, which received Royal Assent on December 12, 2006. The Office's overall objective is to strengthen the fairness, openness and transparency of federal procurement.

The Procurement Ombudsman reports to the Minister of PSPC and is mandated to:

  • Review complaints with respect to the awarding of a contract for the acquisition of goods below $26,400 and services below $105,700 (including taxes);
  • Review complaints with respect to the administration of a contract, regardless of dollar value;
  • Review the practices of departments for acquiring goods and services and make recommendations where necessary to improve those practices;
  • Ensure that an alternative dispute-resolution process is provided, if both parties agree to participate;
  • Provide an alternative dispute-resolution service.

The OPO performs its duties and functions as set out in the Procurement Ombudsman Regulations.

Let’s assume that during the execution of the work, the contractor’s performance is less than stellar. Even though the scope of the contract was quite clear and understood by the contractor at the kick‑off meeting, your client is not satisfied with the work. Some of the issues encountered are as follows:

  • Contractor needs constant guidance;
  • Contractor was late on some deliverables;
  • Quality of deliverables not quite up to par; and
  • Contractor not always present at meetings or often late.

In the end, the contractor reduces the original contract price by more than 60% of the original value, and both parties agree to terminate the contract by mutual consent.

Are there any reasonable measures that can be taken to ensure that the department can rely on its vendors to perform their obligations? Can the department assess a vendor’s performance, and take action to prevent future problems, based on the vendor’s past performance?

The answer is: It depends on whether or not your department or agency has implemented an internal vendor performance policy.

Most departments and agencies have implemented vendor performance policies whereby corrective measures are applied against vendors who do not fulfill their contractual obligations. Such measures can have an impact on a vendor’s ability to bid on future opportunities with or to receive contracts from a department or agency. Vendor performance measures can vary from simple conditions to longer-term debarment.

In departments with existing vendor performance policies, contracting officers must verify the appropriate departmental database or system prior to evaluating bids or awarding contracts to confirm if measures have been applied against any vendor (bidder). Appropriate departmental procedures must be adhered to in cases where measures have been applied against a bidder.

Where PSPC handles procurements on behalf of departments and agencies, PSPC has implemented its Vendor Performance Corrective Measure Policy. This policy standardizes the processes and procedures pertaining to the application of Vendor Performance Corrective Measures (VPCM).
