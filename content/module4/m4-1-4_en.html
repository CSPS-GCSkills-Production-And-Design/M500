Scenario 3

Requirement for R&D Activities

 

You are assigned a contract request for a requirement to conduct Modelling and Simulation (M&S) and Research & Development (R&D) activities related to Human Behaviour Representation (HBR) and Human Performance Modelling (HPM). The request has been given to you in the appropriate format and includes all of the necessary documentation, and formal approvals and signatures. The director responsible for this requirement has allocated a maximum budget of $250,000 for this requirement.

Write your answer and justification in the space provided below, then compare it with the feedback. You may consult the reference material provided.

Question 1 of 2

As the contracting officer (outside PSPC) with delegation of authority to contract for services worth up to $500,000, could you undertake the procurement process for this requirement? Provide the rationale for your decision.

The answer is: Even though you have the delegation of authority to enter into contracts for services worth up to $500,000, as a contracting officer outside PSPC, you would not be authorized to undertake the procurement process for this requirement.

The Common Services Policy lists the Common Services Organizations (CSOs) of the Government of Canada and enumerates the optional and mandatory services provided by these organizations.

Public Services and Procurement Canada (PSPC) operates as a common service agency for the Government of Canada. It provides optional and mandatory services to departments, boards and agencies of the Government of Canada in support of their programs.

PSPC is an optional service provider for the procurement of services. However, under the Common Services Policy, PSPC is a mandatory provider of various procurement-related services set out in the policy, including research and development contracting.

Other mandatory procurement services are identified under Appendix E – Mandatory Services, section 6 of the policy.

Other services provided by PSPC are identified under Appendix F – Optional Services.

PSPC also provides certain specialized supply activities such as:

  • Marine inspection and technical services
  • Cost analysis support
  • Management of Crown-owned production assets
  • Industrial security and personal security screening services for PSPC and industry
  • Development and maintenance of consensus standards and conformity assessment services
  • Management of seized property (assets seized as a result of being obtained from the proceeds of crime)
  • Central freight, travel management, and household goods removal services
  • Transportation advisory services

Let’s assume you are the contracting officer at PSPC who is tasked with undertaking the procurement process for this requirement on behalf of the client department. One day after you get the file, you receive a telephone call from Mr. Bill Bright of Human Behaviour Research Inc. Mr. Bright informs you that he is aware of an upcoming government requirement for R&D services related to human behaviour (referring to the requirement that you are working on). Mr. Bright asks to meet with you so that he can introduce himself and his company. You agree to meet for a brief introductory meeting at the coffee shop in the lobby of your place of work. The meeting goes very well and at the end, as a sign of appreciation, Mr. Bright shakes your hand and gives you a pair of first-row centre-ice tickets to the next home game of the local NHL hockey team. What do you do? Provide the rationale for your decision.

The answer is: You should thank Mr. Bright but give him back the tickets. As stated in Appendix B of the Policy on Conflict of Interest and Post-Employment, regarding gifts, public servants are not to accept any gifts, hospitality or other benefits that may have a real, apparent or potential influence on their objectivity in carrying out their official duties and responsibilities or that may place them under obligation to the donor. This includes activities such as free or discounted admission to sporting and cultural events, travel or conferences.

It should be noted that the Policy on Conflict of Interest and Post-Employment also provides direction and measures to assist organizations and public servants in effectively dealing with real, potential and apparent conflict of interest situations that may arise during and after employment in the public service.

The Values and Ethics Code for the Public Sector outlines the values and expected behaviours that guide public servants in all activities related to their professional duties. By committing to these values and adhering to the expected behaviours, public servants strengthen the ethical culture of the public sector and contribute to public confidence in the integrity of all public institutions.

In addition, public servants must also adhere to the behaviours outlined in their individual organizational code. Organizational codes are consistent with the Code, but outline behaviours specific to the unique mandate and work environment of each department or agency.

The Code of Conduct for Procurement provides all those involved in the procurement process—public servants and vendors alike—with a clear statement of mutual expectations to ensure a common basic understanding among all participants in procurement.

Note: When in doubt, public servants should refer to the requirements found in Appendix B of the Policy on Conflict of Interest and Post-Employment, the Values and Ethics Code for the Public Sector and their organization’s code of conduct to guide appropriate action. Public servants can also seek guidance from their manager and their department’s values and ethics officer.
